Safe Sleep 0-6 Months: NHS Guidelines UK 2026

By ParentPick Editorial Team | Medically reviewed against NHS and Lullaby Trust sources | Updated 20 April 2026

On this page:

6 NHS steps · Back sleeping · 16-20°C · Sleep bags · Mistakes to avoid

Many parents in the UK search for the exact rules for the first six months. According to NHS Start for Life safer-sleep guidance, this period carries the highest risk for sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS), with the peak occurring between 2 and 4 months of age. The Lullaby Trust notes that consistent safer-sleep practices are associated with the significant reduction in UK SIDS rates since the Back to Sleep campaign began in 1991.

This article summarises what UK public health bodies currently publish about sleep position, room-sharing, clear cots, temperature, and alternatives to blankets for babies aged 0-6 months. All recommendations below are attributed to NHS or Lullaby Trust guidance.

What NHS and Lullaby Trust Actually Say

According to NHS Start for Life – Reduce the risk of SIDS (2024), the safest place for a baby to sleep for the first six months is in a separate cot or Moses basket in the same room as parents or carers, for all sleeps day and night. Public health guidance states this should be on a firm, flat, waterproof mattress, with no pillows, duvets, cot bumpers, pods, or loose blankets.

The Lullaby Trust recommends keeping the cot completely clear at all times. According to their safer-sleep advice, a well-fitted baby sleep bag is preferable to loose bedding because it stays in place and cannot cover a baby's face during sleep.

NHS guidance states that babies are to be placed on their back for every sleep. The Lullaby Trust advises that once a baby can roll both ways independently, parents do not need to reposition them, but should continue to place them on their back at the start of each sleep.

Why the First 6 Months Matter Most

According to the Lullaby Trust, babies under six months have developing control of their head, neck, and temperature regulation. Public health guidance states that this developmental stage is associated with an increased vulnerability to overheating and accidental covering of the airway.

NHS advises that overheating is a known risk factor for SIDS. Babies thermoregulate largely through their head and face, which is why guidance recommends keeping the head uncovered during sleep and maintaining a room temperature of 16–20°C.

The Lullaby Trust notes that room-sharing for the first six months is associated with a reduced SIDS risk, as parents are more likely to hear early changes in breathing and babies benefit from the presence of adult breathing sounds and movements.

The 6 Steps Cited by NHS and Lullaby Trust

According to NHS Start for Life, the following measures are recommended for all sleeps in the first six months:

  1. Always on the back. Public health guidance states babies are to be placed on their back for every sleep, day and night. The Lullaby Trust advises that side sleeping is not recommended, as babies can roll onto their stomach. NHS guidance states that babies who can roll both ways independently do not need to be repositioned, but should always be started on their back.
  2. Feet to foot of cot. NHS guidance states positioning baby with feet at the foot of the cot or Moses basket helps prevent wriggling down under any bedding. The Lullaby Trust recommends this for both cots and Moses baskets.
  3. Clear cot, always. According to The Lullaby Trust, the cot should contain a firm, flat, waterproof mattress with a fitted sheet only. Public health guidance states no pillows, duvets, bumpers, toys, pods, nests, or loose blankets. NHS advises that the mattress should fit snugly with no gaps at the sides.
  4. Same room, separate sleep space. NHS advises room-sharing for the first six months for all sleeps. The Lullaby Trust recommends a separate cot or Moses basket next to the parental bed, not bed-sharing on a sofa or armchair. Public health guidance states that sofa or armchair sharing carries a significantly increased risk.
  5. Smoke-free environment. NHS guidance states that smoking increases SIDS risk and babies should not be exposed to tobacco smoke in the home, car, or on clothing. The Lullaby Trust advises that vaping data is still emerging and recommends treating it the same as smoking until more evidence is available.
  6. Keep room 16-20°C. According to NHS Start for Life, this is described as the ideal range. The Lullaby Trust recommends using a room thermometer rather than estimating by touch. Public health guidance states that overheating is a known risk factor.

Back Sleeping: Why Position Matters

According to the Lullaby Trust, back sleeping is associated with a reduced risk of SIDS compared to front or side sleeping. Public health guidance states that babies placed on their backs are less likely to rebreathe exhaled carbon dioxide and less likely to overheat.

NHS guidance states that babies will often turn their heads to the side during sleep, which is normal and safe. The Lullaby Trust advises that it is the body position that matters, not head orientation. According to NHS, babies should not be placed on their side with a rolled towel or positioner to keep them in place, as these products are not recommended.

The Lullaby Trust notes that the Back to Sleep campaign in 1991 was associated with an 80% reduction in UK SIDS rates within a decade. Public health guidance states that this remains the single most important safer-sleep measure.

Clear Cot: What NHS Means by 'Nothing Else'

According to NHS Start for Life, a clear cot means a firm, flat, waterproof mattress with a tightly fitted sheet only. The Lullaby Trust specifically advises against pillows, duvets, quilts, cot bumpers, soft toys, pods, nests, and loose blankets for babies under 12 months.

Public health guidance states that cot bumpers are not recommended, including so-called breathable mesh versions. The Lullaby Trust notes that bumpers increase the risk of entanglement, overheating, and were originally designed for drafty Victorian homes, not modern centrally heated houses.

NHS advises that second-hand mattresses should be avoided if possible. The Lullaby Trust states that if a second-hand mattress must be used, it should be from a smoke-free home, be firm, flat, clean, waterproof, with no cracks or tears. Public health guidance notes a slight increase in SIDS risk is associated with second-hand mattresses.

Room-Sharing for 6 Months: NHS Guidance

According to NHS Start for Life, babies should sleep in the same room as a parent or carer for the first six months, for daytime naps and night-time sleeps. The Lullaby Trust states this is associated with up to a 50% reduction in SIDS risk.

Public health guidance states that room-sharing does not mean bed-sharing. NHS advises that the safest place is a separate cot or Moses basket next to the adult bed. The Lullaby Trust specifically warns against sleeping with a baby on a sofa or armchair, as this carries the highest risk.

NHS guidance states that after six months, parents may choose to move baby to their own room. The Lullaby Trust notes that SIDS risk reduces significantly after six months, though safer-sleep practices remain important. For the next stage, see safe sleep 6-12 months.

Temperature Control: 16-20°C

According to NHS Start for Life, an ideal room temperature for baby sleep is 16–20°C. The Lullaby Trust recommends using a digital room thermometer placed away from radiators and direct sunlight, rather than estimating by feeling the air.

Public health guidance states that overheating is a known SIDS risk factor. NHS advises checking a baby's chest or back of the neck to assess warmth – these areas should feel warm, not hot or sweaty. The Lullaby Trust notes that cold hands and feet are normal and not a reliable indicator of core temperature.

NHS guidance states that babies do not need hats indoors for sleep after the initial post-birth period, as they lose excess heat through their head. The Lullaby Trust advises removing hats once indoors and maintaining the 16-20°C range.

Blankets vs Sleep Bags for Newborns

NHS guidance states that both thin cellular blankets and baby sleep bags can be used if used correctly for babies under 12 months. The Lullaby Trust recommends a baby sleep bag as the first choice because it cannot cover the face and maintains a more consistent temperature.

Public health guidance states that if a blanket is used, it should be thin, cellular, and firmly tucked no higher than the chest, with baby feet-to-foot. NHS advises not to place a blanket over a sleep bag. The Lullaby Trust notes that tucking often loosens during the night.

According to the Lullaby Trust, sleep bags with a fitted neck and armholes are preferable to loose bedding. NHS guidance states that blankets, including cellular blankets, are not recommended under 12 months, with sleep bags cited as the safer alternative. Swaddling: What NHS Says

NHS does not recommend for or against swaddling. The Lullaby Trust states that if parents choose to swaddle, it should be with thin cotton muslin, stopped at the first signs of rolling (usually 8-12 weeks), and never combined with a sleep bag due to overheating risk.

Public health guidance states not to swaddle above the shoulders, not to place a swaddled baby on their front or side, and not to swaddle if bed-sharing. NHS advises that once rolling begins, swaddling must stop immediately and transition to a sleep bag.

Daytime Naps: Same Rules Apply

According to the Lullaby Trust, the same safer-sleep rules apply for daytime naps as for night-time sleeps. NHS guidance states that every sleep counts in the first six months.

Public health guidance states that car seats, bouncers, swings, and pods are not designed for prolonged sleep. The Lullaby Trust advises that babies should be transferred to a flat cot or Moses basket as soon as possible, as the scrunched position can compromise the airway.

NHS advises that for naps downstairs, a Moses basket in the same room as a parent or carer is the safest option, maintaining the clear cot rules and back-sleeping position.

Products NHS Advises to Avoid

The Lullaby Trust specifically advises against products marketed to prevent SIDS, including wedges, positioners, sleep pods, nests, and cot bumpers. Public health guidance states none are proven to reduce risk and many increase suffocation or overheating risk.

NHS guidance states that breathing monitors, movement mats, and wearable trackers are not recommended for healthy babies, as they have not been shown to prevent SIDS and may provide false reassurance. The Lullaby Trust notes that these devices do not replace safer-sleep practices.

According to NHS, weighted blankets, weighted sleep bags, and weighted swaddles are not to be used for babies under 12 months under any circumstances due to overheating and restricted movement risks.

Common Mistakes to Avoid (0-6 Months)

  • NHS advises not to place baby on their front or side to sleep, even if they appear to prefer it.
  • The Lullaby Trust recommends not using loose blankets, including cellular blankets, for babies under 12 months.
  • Public health guidance states not to let baby get too hot – avoid over-layering, hats indoors, or room temperatures above 20°C.
  • NHS guidance states not to smoke around baby or in the home, as this increases SIDS risk threefold.
  • The Lullaby Trust advises not to sleep with baby on a sofa or armchair, with or without a pillow or blanket.
  • Public health guidance states not to use cot bumpers, even mesh ones, as they pose entanglement risk.
  • NHS advises not to place a blanket over a sleep bag or combine swaddling with a sleep bag.

Feeding to Sleep: NHS View

According to NHS Start for Life, feeding a baby to sleep is normal and common. The Lullaby Trust notes that breastfeeding is associated with a reduced SIDS risk.

Public health guidance states that if parents may fall asleep during feeding, they should avoid sofas and armchairs and instead feed in a clear adult bed, removing pillows and loose bedding. NHS advises that if co-sleeping occurs, baby should be placed back in their own clear cot before the parent falls asleep.

The Lullaby Trust recommends that parents who smoke, have consumed alcohol, or take sedating medication should not share a bed with their baby under any circumstances.

After 6 Months: What Changes

According to NHS, SIDS risk reduces significantly after six months. The Lullaby Trust notes that after six months, parents may choose to move baby to their own room if desired, though many families continue room-sharing longer.

Public health guidance continues to advise no pillows or duvets until at least 12 months. The Lullaby Trust recommends that from 12 months, a small light blanket may be introduced if parents wish, though sleep bags often remain the preferred option. See when can baby have a pillow.

NHS guidance states that once baby can roll both ways confidently, parents do not need to reposition them if they roll onto their tummy during sleep, but should continue to start them on their back.

In summary, UK public health bodies are consistent: according to NHS Start for Life and Lullaby Trust guidance, babies aged 0-6 months should sleep on their back, in a clear cot, in the same room as a parent or carer, at 16-20°C, without loose blankets. A correctly sized sleep bag is cited as the safer alternative to blankets for this age group. Parents with specific concerns about prematurity, low birth weight, or medical conditions are advised by the NHS to seek personalised advice from a health visitor, midwife or GP.
