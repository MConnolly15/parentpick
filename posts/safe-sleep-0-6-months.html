Safe Sleep Checklist: 0-6 Months (NHS)

The six rules that cut SIDS risk by 80% in the UK – and how we actually used them.

Written by Mark, Belfast dad of two • Checked against NHS.uk and Lullaby Trust • Last updated 16 April 2026

In Northern Ireland, around 4-5 babies die from SIDS each year. Across the UK, it's about 200 babies annually – down from 1,500 in 1991 when the Back to Sleep campaign started. The first six months are the highest risk period, peaking at 2-4 months. The NHS and Lullaby Trust guidance hasn't changed because it works.

UK SIDS rates dropped 80% since 1991, largely due to back-sleeping and clear cots. These six steps are proven, not opinion.

We followed this exact checklist for both our boys in our Belfast terrace. It felt strict at first, but after two weeks it was automatic. Here's what NHS actually says, without the leaflet jargon.

The 6 non-negotiables (NHS)

1. Always on their back – for every sleep, day and night. Not side, not tummy. Back sleeping reduces SIDS risk by up to 50% according to Lullaby Trust data. If they roll themselves later (usually after 5-6 months), that's fine, but always start them on their back. We had a sign above the cot for grandparents: "BACK ONLY".

2. Feet to foot of cot – baby's feet at the bottom of the Moses basket or cot. This stops them wriggling down under blankets and overheating. In a Moses basket, it means their head is near the top with space at feet. In a cot, feet touching the foot end.

3. Clear cot, always – mattress only with fitted sheet. No pillows, duvets, bumpers, toys, pods, nests, sleep positioners, or loose blankets. The mattress should be firm, flat, waterproof, and fit snugly with no gaps. We bought a new mattress for each baby – second-hand mattresses increase SIDS risk slightly.

4. Same room, separate sleep space – NHS advises room-sharing for the first 6 months for all sleeps, day and night. Not in your bed, not on sofa – in their own cot or Moses basket next to your bed. Room-sharing reduces SIDS risk by up to 50%. We kept the Moses basket right next to the bed for 5 months, then moved to cot in our room until 6.5 months.

5. No smoking, ever – around baby, in the house, in the car, or on your clothes. Smoking increases SIDS risk 3-fold. If you smoke, baby should sleep in separate room from you (but still in same room as non-smoking adult). Vaping data is still emerging – Lullaby Trust advises treating it same as smoking until we know more.

6. Keep room 16-20°C – not too hot, not too cold. Use a room thermometer, not your hand on their forehead. Overheating is a major risk factor. In UK winter with central heating, this is harder than it sounds – we kept heating at 18°C overnight and used appropriate TOG.

Back sleeping – why it matters so much

Before 1991, UK parents were told to put babies on their fronts. SIDS rates were 1,500+ per year. The Back to Sleep campaign changed advice based on research from New Zealand and Netherlands. Within 5 years, UK SIDS deaths dropped to under 400. The mechanism: babies on their backs are less likely to rebreathe exhaled CO2, less likely to overheat, and have better arousal from sleep.

Practical tip: babies will try to turn their heads to side – that's normal and safe. It's the body position that matters. If they hate being on their back, try swaddling for first 8-12 weeks (stopping at first signs of rolling), white noise, and ensuring they're not overtired.

Blankets vs sleep bags – NHS view

NHS says both are safe if used correctly, but sleep bags are easier and safer for most parents. If using blankets: use one thin cellular blanket only, tucked in firmly below shoulders, feet to foot, no higher than chest level. Never use duvets, quilts, or weighted blankets under 12 months.

We switched to a 2.5 TOG sleep bag at 3 weeks because blankets kept coming untucked. Sleep bags can't cover the face, maintain consistent temperature, and are what NHS health visitors now recommend first-line. Room-sharing for 6 months – the reality

NHS guidance is clear: baby sleeps in same room as you for first 6 months, for all sleeps including daytime naps. This is the hardest rule for many parents, especially when returning to work and wanting baby in their own room.

Why 6 months? SIDS risk is highest 2-4 months, remains elevated until 6 months, then drops significantly. Room-sharing means you hear early breathing changes, and baby benefits from your breathing sounds and movements which help regulate their own.

We managed it by: keeping Moses basket downstairs for daytime naps in living room (with us), and cot in our bedroom at night. Yes, sleep was broken. Yes, it was worth it. After 6 months we moved him to his own room and both slept better.

Temperature control in UK homes

The 16-20°C range is tricky in UK housing. In winter, central heating often pushes bedrooms to 22-24°C. In summer heatwaves, rooms can hit 26°C+.

Our Belfast setup: room thermometer (£5 from Boots) on wall away from radiator. Heating on timer: off 10pm-6am, set to 18°C. In winter we used 2.5 TOG sleep bag + sleepsuit + vest. In summer heatwave 2024 (room 24°C), we used 0.5 TOG bag + just nappy.

Check baby's chest or back of neck – should feel warm, not hot or sweaty. Cold hands and feet are normal. Never use hats indoors for sleep – babies lose excess heat through heads.

What about swaddling?

NHS doesn't recommend for or against swaddling, but says if you do it: use thin cotton, stop at first signs of rolling (usually 8-12 weeks, sometimes earlier), never swaddle above shoulders, never put swaddled baby on front or side, and don't swaddle if baby shares your bed.

We swaddled our first for 9 weeks, second refused it. For our first we used the Love to Dream Swaddle UP 1.0 Tog because it allows arms-up position (babies self-soothe by sucking hands) and is safer than loose muslin that can come undone. It's what our health visitor suggested when we struggled with traditional swaddling. Stop swaddling completely by 12 weeks maximum, then transition to sleep bag.

Daytime naps – same rules apply

Every sleep counts, not just nights. The same six rules apply for daytime naps. Biggest mistake UK parents make: letting baby nap in car seat, bouncer, or swing for hours. These are not designed for prolonged sleep – baby's airway can become compromised in scrunched position.

Move baby to flat cot or Moses basket as soon as you get home. For naps downstairs, we used a second-hand Moses basket in living room – same clear cot rules, in same room as us.

Products to avoid (NHS warning)

Lullaby Trust specifically warns against products claiming to prevent SIDS: wedges, positioners, pods, nests, cot bumpers, weighted blankets, and breathing monitors. None are proven to reduce SIDS and many increase risk by creating suffocation hazards or giving false reassurance.

Cot bumpers are particularly dangerous – they were designed to stop drafts in Victorian times, not for modern centrally heated homes. They increase risk of entanglement and overheating, and are banned in some countries. Don't use them, even the "breathable" mesh ones.

Feeding to sleep – what NHS says

It's normal and common to feed baby to sleep. NHS says it's fine, but be aware of the risks if you might fall asleep during feeding. Never feed on sofa or armchair where you could doze – risk of baby slipping into cushions is 50 times higher than bed-sharing.

Safe approach: feed in upright chair, bright light on, phone timer set for 20 minutes. If you feel sleepy, put baby back in cot first. See our full feeding to sleep guide for practical tips.

Common questions

What if baby rolls onto tummy?

Once baby can roll both ways confidently (usually 5-6 months), you don't need to flip them back. But always start them on their back. Keep cot clear so if they roll, there's nothing to suffocate against.

Do dummies reduce SIDS?

Some research suggests yes, but NHS doesn't recommend them specifically for SIDS prevention. If you use one, don't force it, and if breastfeeding, wait until feeding is established (around 4 weeks).

Can I use a second-hand mattress?

NHS advises against it if possible. If you must, ensure it's firm, flat, clean, waterproof, with no cracks or tears, and from a smoke-free home. Risk increases slightly with second-hand mattresses, possibly due to bacteria or loss of firmness.

After 6 months – what changes

SIDS risk drops significantly after 6 months, and again after 12 months. At 6 months you can: move baby to their own room if you want, allow tummy sleeping if they roll themselves, introduce a comfort object (small muslin). Keep the clear cot until 12 months minimum – no pillows or duvets until at least 12 months, ideally 18-24 months.
