Nappy Rash Cream: NHS Guide 2026

What the NHS actually recommends, and how UK parents use barrier creams day-to-day.

Written by Mark, Belfast dad • Checked against NHS guidance • Medically reviewed against NHS.uk April 2026

NHS guidance on nappy rash hasn't changed in years because it works: keep skin clean and dry, change frequently, use a thin barrier cream. In the UK, 1 in 3 babies get nappy rash before 12 months, usually between 9-12 months when diet changes and teeth come through. The cream doesn't cure it – your routine does.

What barrier cream actually does

Think waterproof jacket, not medicine. Zinc oxide (Sudocrem), titanium dioxide (Metanium), or dexpanthenol (Bepanthen) create a thin layer that stops wee and poo touching skin. NHS specifically recommends "thin layer of barrier preparation" at each change for babies prone to rash. It doesn't treat infection, it prevents the burn.

We learned this the hard way – our first got rash at 8 months during weaning. Health visitor said we were using too much cream, too infrequently. Changed to pea-sized amount every change, cleared in 48 hours.

NHS step-by-step application

This is the method NHS health visitors teach in UK clinics:

  • Wash hands, lay baby on clean mat
  • Remove dirty nappy, clean front to back with water or fragrance-free wipes
  • Pat dry with soft towel – don't rub sore skin. Air dry for 2 minutes if possible
  • Scoop pea-sized cream on fingertip
  • Dot on cheeks, then spread thinly – you should see skin through it
  • Cover all skin that gets wet, including folds and around genitals
  • Put clean nappy on, not too tight
  • Wash hands

Frequency matters more than brand. NHS says change every 2-3 hours daytime, once overnight unless poo, and apply barrier each time if prone.

Which cream for which situation

Daily prevention (no rash): Sudocrem Antiseptic Healing Cream or Bepanthen Nappy Care. Both available in Boots, Superdrug, Tesco for £3-6. Use thin layer at every change.

Red, sore, but not broken skin: Metanium Nappy Rash Ointment. Thicker barrier, better for active irritation. Apply 3-4 times daily thinly. £5.50 for 30g at Boots April 2026.

Very sensitive or eczema-prone: Bepanthen Sensiderm or plain petroleum jelly (Vaseline). Fragrance-free, fewer ingredients. NHS recommends avoiding fragranced products for eczema babies.

What NHS doesn't recommend: Talcum powder (inhalation risk), antiseptic adult creams like Germolene, steroid creams without prescription, home remedies like cornflour.

UK climate factors

UK winter – central heating dries skin, making rash worse. Use slightly more cream, ensure room isn't over 20°C. Summer – heat and sweat increase friction. Change more frequently, use nappy-free time daily. Weaning – acidic poos from fruit cause rash spikes. Increase barrier frequency during weaning weeks.

When to see GP – NHS red flags

See GP or health visitor if: rash lasts more than 3 days despite correct barrier use, skin is blistered, bleeding or oozing yellow pus, baby has fever or seems unwell, rash has sharp bright red border with spots outside main area (possible thrush), or rash covers large area and baby is in pain. Don't use over-the-counter antifungal without advice – thrush needs specific treatment.

Prevention routine that works

Based on NHS and what worked for us: 1) Change before and after naps, 2) Use water and cotton wool for newborns, switch to fragrance-free wipes at 1 month, 3) Nappy-free time 10 minutes daily on towel (we did after morning change), 4) Avoid tight nappies and plastic pants, 5) Wash cloth nappies at 60°C with non-bio detergent, 6) No baby wipes with alcohol or fragrance.

FAQs

Can I use Sudocrem at every nappy change?

Yes, NHS says thin barrier cream at each change is fine for prevention. Use pea-sized amount, not thick layer.

Does barrier cream make rash worse?

Only if applied too thickly or on dirty skin. Thin layer on clean, dry skin prevents rash. Thick layers trap moisture.

When should I stop using nappy cream?

Many parents stop around 18-24 months when nappy use reduces. No medical need to stop if skin is healthy.

Is Metanium better than Sudocrem?

Metanium is thicker, better for active sore rash. Sudocrem is better for daily prevention. See our full comparison.
