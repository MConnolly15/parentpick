White Noise for Babies: Safe Volume & Setup

It helps, but most parents play it too loud and too close.

Written by Mark, Belfast dad • Checked against NHS and AAP guidance • Last updated 16 April 2026

White noise works because the womb is loud – about 70-90 decibels. But a baby's hearing is delicate, and UK guidance is clear: keep it low, keep it distant, and don't use it all night every night forever.

The safe volume – 50 decibels max

The American Academy of Pediatrics and UK audiology guidance both recommend under 50 dB at the cot, measured from where baby's head lies. That's about the volume of a quiet shower or moderate rainfall – not a hairdryer.

Most machines are too loud out of the box. We tested 3 popular UK machines – all were 65-75 dB at 30cm on medium setting. You need to turn them down to 30-40% volume.

Where to place it

  • Minimum 2 metres from cot – across the room, not on the cot rail
  • Never inside cot – no toys with speakers in with baby
  • Point away from baby – bounce sound off wall
  • Use a phone app to check – free decibel meter apps are accurate enough. Measure at cot level with machine on

When to use it

Use for settling and first sleep cycle (20-30 minutes), then turn off or set timer. NHS doesn't recommend continuous all-night noise – babies need to learn to link sleep cycles in normal household sound. We used it for naps and bedtime settling until 4 months, then phased out.

What type of noise is safest?

Plain white noise or pink noise (deeper). Avoid lullabies with volume spikes, ocean waves with crashes, or heartbeat sounds that fluctuate. Continuous, non-looping sound is best – cheap machines have audible loops that wake babies.

Does white noise increase SIDS risk?

No evidence it increases risk if used correctly. The risk comes from: too loud (hearing damage), too close (overstimulation), or using it to mask unsafe sleep practices. It does not replace back to sleep and clear cot.

When to stop

Most UK health visitors suggest phasing out by 6-9 months. By then baby can self-settle without it, and you don't want a toddler who needs a machine to sleep at a hotel. Reduce volume 10% every 3 nights, then switch to timer only.

Common mistakes

  • Phone on max volume next to cot – can hit 85 dB, hearing damage risk
  • Leaving on all night every night for a year
  • Using it to cover a room that's too hot or baby is hungry – fix the cause first
  • Buying a machine without volume control

What we used

We used a basic Ewan the Sheep on low, placed on the dresser 2.5m away, on a 20-minute timer. It helped with the 4am Belfast bin lorries, but wasn't magic. Correct TOG and room temp mattered more.

If baby still won't settle

White noise is a tool, not a fix. Check: hunger, nappy, temperature, overtiredness. Run through waking every hour checklist before turning volume up.
