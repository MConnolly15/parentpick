Feeding to Sleep: NHS View

It's normal, not a failure. Here's when and how to change it.

Written by Mark, Belfast dad • Checked against NHS guidance • Last updated 16 April 2026

NHS and breastfeeding guidance is clear: feeding to sleep is biologically normal in the first 6 months. It helps babies settle, regulates blood sugar, and supports milk supply. The problem isn't the feeding – it's when baby can't fall back asleep without it every 45 minutes all night.

Why babies feed to sleep

Milk contains sleep-inducing hormones. Sucking is calming. In the early months, it's the most efficient way to get baby to sleep. NHS breastfeeding support says don't worry about "bad habits" before 4 months – focus on establishing feeding.

When it becomes a challenge

Around 4-6 months, sleep cycles mature. Baby wakes briefly between cycles (normal). If they've always fallen asleep feeding, they need feeding again to go back. That's when parents report waking every hour. It's not hunger – it's a sleep association.

NHS approach – responsive, not rigid

NHS doesn't recommend cry-it-out or strict sleep training under 6 months. Instead: 1) Ensure daytime calories are enough, 2) Create a consistent bedtime routine, 3) Gradually reduce the feed-to-sleep link.

Gentle ways to reduce (6+ months)

1. Move feed earlier in routine

Feed in living room with lights on, then upstairs for nappy, sleep bag, story, bed. Breaks the direct feed-sleep link.

2. Pause before responding

When baby stirs, wait 30-60 seconds. Often they'll resettle without feed. If crying escalates, respond.

3. Partner resettles

For one waking per night, non-feeding parent settles with patting/shushing. Baby learns other ways to fall back asleep.

4. Reduce feed time

If feeding 15 minutes, reduce by 2 minutes every 3 nights. Unlatch before fully asleep, finish settling in cot.

What not to do

  • Don't night-wean before 6 months without health visitor advice
  • Don't replace feeds with water under 6 months
  • Don't bed-share to make feeding easier if you smoke, drank alcohol, or are excessively tired – see safe sleep guidance

How we did it

We fed to sleep until 5 months. Then we moved the bedtime feed to before bath. It took 10 nights of extra settling, but night wakings dropped from 4 to 1. We kept the 2am feed until 9 months – NHS says that's normal.

If you're breastfeeding

Feeding to sleep protects supply. If you want to reduce, do it gradually and watch nappies/weight gain. Speak to your health visitor or local breastfeeding support – don't go cold turkey.
