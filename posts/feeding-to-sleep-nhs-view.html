Feeding to Sleep: What the NHS Says

It's normal – here's how to do it safely.

Written by Mark, Belfast dad • Checked against NHS guidance • Last updated 16 April 2026

Feeding to sleep is biologically normal, especially for breastfed babies under 6 months. The NHS does not say you must stop. The risk comes from where baby falls asleep, not the feeding itself.

Safe way to feed to sleep

1. Feed in a chair or bed where you cannot fall asleep accidentally. 2. When baby is drowsy but awake, transfer to a clear cot on their back. 3. Keep room 16-20°C and use the right TOG – see our TOG guide.

What to avoid

Never feed to sleep on a sofa or armchair – NHS data shows this greatly increases SIDS risk if you doze off. Avoid falling asleep in bed after alcohol, smoking, or sedating medication. Don't prop bottles or leave baby unattended with a bottle.

Does it create a bad habit?

No evidence from NHS guidance suggests feeding to sleep causes long-term sleep problems. Many babies naturally grow out of it between 6-12 months. If you want to change it later, do so gradually and keep safe sleep rules the same.
