When Can Baby Sleep With a Blanket? NHS Guidance UK 2026

By ParentPick Editorial Team | Reviewed against NHS and Lullaby Trust sources | Updated April 2026

Many parents in the UK ask when a blanket becomes safe. According to NHS Start for Life safer-sleep guidance, the position for the first year is consistent: babies do not need a blanket, and loose bedding is best avoided. The Lullaby Trust recommends using a baby sleep bag instead of any blanket for babies under 12 months.

This article summarises what UK public health bodies currently publish about blankets, cellular blankets, sleep bags, and room temperature. All recommendations below are attributed to NHS or Lullaby Trust guidance.

What NHS and Lullaby Trust Actually Say

According to NHS Start for Life – Reduce the risk of SIDS (2024), the safest place for a baby to sleep for the first six months is in a separate cot or Moses basket in the same room as parents or carers, on a firm, flat, waterproof mattress, with no pillows, duvets, cot bumpers, pods, or loose blankets.

The Lullaby Trust recommends keeping the cot completely clear. Public health guidance states that a well-fitted baby sleep bag is preferable to loose bedding because it stays in place and cannot cover a baby's face.

NHS guidance states not to place a blanket over a sleep bag. The Lullaby Trust also advises against combining swaddling with a sleep bag due to overheating risk. For more on the first six months, see safe sleep 0–6 months.

Why 12 Months Is the Minimum Age Cited

According to the Lullaby Trust, babies under 12 months have limited strength and coordination to push loose fabric away from their nose and mouth. Public health guidance states that this increases the risk of accidental covering of the face, which is associated with overheating and suffocation.

NHS advises that overheating is a known risk factor for sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS). Babies thermoregulate largely through their head and face, which is why guidance recommends keeping the head uncovered during sleep.

The Lullaby Trust notes that after around 12 months, most babies can roll both ways, sit up, and free themselves from bedding more reliably. This developmental change is why UK guidance cites 12 months as the earliest point to consider a light blanket, not as a requirement to introduce one. Further detail for this age band is summarised in safe sleep 6–12 months.

Are Cellular Blankets Considered Safe?

Cellular cotton blankets were historically mentioned because the holes allow airflow. According to current Lullaby Trust safer-sleep advice, even lightweight cellular blankets are not recommended for babies under 12 months.

Public health guidance states that if parents choose to use a blanket before 12 months against current advice, it would need to be thin, cellular, and firmly tucked in no higher than the armpits, with baby placed feet-to-foot. However, the Lullaby Trust recommends a sleep bag instead, as tucking can loosen during the night.

NHS guidance states that thick blankets, fleece throws, weighted blankets, and quilts are not to be used for babies under 12 months under any circumstances.

What to Use Instead of a Blanket (0–12 Months)

The Lullaby Trust recommends a baby sleep bag suitable for the baby's weight and with a fitted neck and armholes. According to NHS guidance, sleep bags remove the need for loose bedding and help maintain a more consistent temperature.

Public health guidance states that babies are to be placed on their back for every sleep, with feet to the foot of the cot, on a firm flat mattress, and in the same room as a parent or carer for the first six months, day and night.

NHS guidance states not to place a blanket over a sleep bag. The Lullaby Trust also advises that hats are not to be worn indoors for sleep after the initial post-birth period.

How to Keep Baby Warm Safely Without a Blanket

According to NHS Start for Life, an ideal room temperature for baby sleep is 16–20°C. The Lullaby Trust recommends using a room thermometer rather than estimating by touch of the air.

Public health guidance states that layering with a vest and sleepsuit under a sleep bag is usually sufficient. NHS advises that babies do not need extra bedding because they cannot regulate temperature as adults do. See also room temperature guidance.

When It Is Considered Safe to Introduce a Blanket

According to the Lullaby Trust, from 12 months onwards parents may choose to introduce a small, light, breathable blanket if they wish to move away from a sleep bag. NHS guidance does not mandate a blanket at any age, and many families continue with sleep bags into toddlerhood.

Public health guidance states that any first blanket after 12 months should remain below shoulder height and must not cover the head. The Lullaby Trust recommends continuing to keep pillows, duvets and cot bumpers out of the cot.

Step-by-Step: Introducing First Blanket After 12 Months

  1. According to NHS guidance, parents are advised to continue placing baby on their back to sleep for the first year, and onward if tolerated.
  2. The Lullaby Trust recommends using a firm, flat mattress with a fitted sheet only, in a clear cot.
  3. Public health guidance states to position the child feet-to-foot and tuck a thin cellular blanket across the chest, no higher than the armpits.
  4. NHS advises ensuring the head remains uncovered during sleep.
  5. The Lullaby Trust recommends avoiding weighted or heavy blankets for toddlers, as these increase overheating risk.

When Can a Baby Have a Duvet or Quilt?

According to NHS Start for Life, duvets, quilts and pillows are not to be used for babies under 12 months due to suffocation and overheating risk. The Lullaby Trust recommends that a duvet is not introduced before 12 months, and many UK health visiting teams advise waiting until at least 18 months, choosing a low-tog toddler duvet (around 4.0 TOG) with no pillow.

Public health guidance states that adult bedding is not suitable for infants. For pillow-specific timing, see when can baby have a pillow – NHS view.

Common Mistakes Parents Are Advised to Avoid

  • NHS advises not to use loose blankets, throws, or muslins as covers for babies under 12 months during sleep.
  • The Lullaby Trust recommends not placing a blanket over a baby sleep bag, as this combination increases overheating risk.
  • Public health guidance states not to overdress a baby under a high-TOG bag; more layers do not equal safer.
  • NHS guidance states not to use electric blankets, hot water bottles, or to position cots next to radiators or heaters.
  • The Lullaby Trust advises not to let a baby sleep on a sofa or armchair, with or without a blanket, due to a significantly increased SIDS risk.
  • Public health guidance states not to use cot bumpers, sleep pods, or nests as an alternative to blankets.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. When can a baby sleep with a blanket according to NHS guidance?

According to NHS Start for Life and the Lullaby Trust, loose blankets are not recommended for babies under 12 months. Public health guidance states a well-fitted sleep bag is the safer option for this age group.

2. Can a newborn sleep with a cellular blanket?

The Lullaby Trust advises against any loose bedding for newborns, including cellular blankets. NHS guidance states newborns should sleep in a clear cot with no blanket, using a sleep bag if additional warmth is needed.

3. Is a sleep bag safer than a blanket?

According to the Lullaby Trust, yes for under-12s. A correctly sized sleep bag cannot ride up over the face. NHS guidance states not to place a blanket over a sleep bag. For age-specific guidance on using sleep bags safely in the first six months, see safe sleep 0-6 months.

4. What TOG for a 19°C room?

Guidance published by the Lullaby Trust and NHS Start for Life suggests a 2.5 TOG bag with a vest and sleepsuit for 18–20°C, which is described as the ideal range.

5. When can a toddler have a duvet?

NHS guidance advises no duvets under 12 months. The Lullaby Trust recommends waiting until at least 12 months for a blanket and considering a low-tog toddler duvet from around 18 months, following manufacturer age guidance.

In summary, UK public health bodies are consistent: according to NHS and Lullaby Trust guidance, babies under 12 months should not sleep with a blanket. A sleep bag, clear cot, back-sleeping, and a room temperature of 16–20°C are the measures most frequently cited. Parents with specific concerns about temperature, prematurity, or medical conditions are advised by the NHS to seek personalised advice from a health visitor, midwife or GP.
